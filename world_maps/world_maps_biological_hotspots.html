Biological Hotspots

Biodiversity hotspots are defined as areas "[h]aving at least 1500 endemic plant species and having lost at least 70 per cent of their original habitat extent". 1 Together, the 35 hotspots contain at least 50% of the world's total plant species and 42% of the worldâ€™s terrestrial vertebrates as endemic. Originally representing 16% of the Earth's surface, the unique habitat of the hotspots has diminished to just 2.3%. 2 Consequently, conservation funding, research and activism have primarily focused on the urgency of preserving this remaining biodiversity to reduce the risk of extinction of "more than half of our natural heritage." 3 Comprising 70% of the world's known languages, many of which are predicted to disappear by century's end, the hotspots are also remarkable for their cultural diversity, a vital clue perhaps, to our intertwined fates. 4

There are 144 nations under whose jurisdiction the hotspots collectively fall. As this atlas shows 14 of the 35 hotspots have reached or surpassed the Convention on Biological Diversity's (CBD) 2020 terrestrial protected area target of 17%, and 21 have not. However, the protected areas are also supposed to be representative of a nation's different ecoregions. The hotspots comprise 398 ecoregions and of these, only 179 have reached or surpassed the 17% target. 201 have not.

There are 420 major cities in the hotspots (over 300,000 inhabitants), 244 of which are forecast to sprawl into extant, high biodiversity habitat.
